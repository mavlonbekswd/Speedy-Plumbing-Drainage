# Hidden Waterways — hero art direction

*The design philosophy governing the cinematic hero of Speedy Plumbing & Drain.
Written first, expressed second: every frame of the 3D sequence answers to this page.*

## The movement

**Hidden Waterways** is about revealing the invisible infrastructure beneath ordinary
comfort. Every home rests on a buried river — copper and steel veins that carry water
silently through darkness until it arrives, composed and obedient, at a polished tap.
The aesthetic honours both halves of that journey: the industrial dark where the work
happens, and the domestic light where the result is enjoyed. One continuous camera
breath connects them, because in reality they *are* connected — that is the entire
promise of the trade.

## Space and form

The journey moves through two opposing volumes that must feel like one world. The pipe
interior is a cathedral of pressure: curved, rib-vaulted, metallic, lit only by what
travels through it. The kitchen is its resolution: orthogonal, calm, generous with
negative space. Form language shifts from cylindrical to rectilinear as the story
resolves — turbulence becoming order. Nothing in either space is decorative; every
flange, every counter edge exists because a real plumber or a real kitchen would have
it. The transition between them is not a cut but a passage through light, the way
water itself experiences emerging from a spout.

## Color and material

One palette, two temperatures. The pipe lives in deep charcoal-navy (#0b0d10 →
#232b36) with steel that actually behaves like steel — environment-reflected, never
painted grey. The single accent is the brand's electric blue (#38bdf8), used the way
a master colourist uses it: carried *by the water*, never sprayed as ambient neon.
The kitchen answers in neutral warmth — matte stone, brushed stainless, chrome that
earns its highlights from a believable room. Water is the only element permitted
translucency and glow, because water is the protagonist. Restraint here is the
craft: the difference between premium and gaudy is perhaps twenty percent opacity.

## Motion and rhythm

Motion is directional and patient. Water always moves — even in the idle frame the
river runs, because a still hero is a dead hero — but the camera never hurries; it
travels like a slow dolly on rails, eased by scroll, breathing when the user rests.
Rhythm comes from repetition passing the lens: ribs, joints, streaks, then ripples.
The pour at the end must feel like exhaling — a thin, clean, confident stream, not a
spectacle. Every easing curve is to be tuned by hand until it feels inevitable, the
way a title sequence by a great studio feels inevitable.

## Hierarchy and the written word

Typography stays sovereign. The oversized headline is the loudest object on screen
and the scene exists beneath it, darkened where they meet, never competing. Text is
sparse and load-bearing — a headline, a promise, two actions. The 3D sequence is the
subtext, not the paragraph. Calls to action sit above everything, untouched by canvas
or particle, clickable in every frame of the story.

## The standard

This must read as the work of someone at the top of their field — meticulously
crafted, laboured over, every material value and light intensity revisited until the
whole holds together like a single photograph. No cartoon physics, no game-engine
flash, no shortcut that shows. If a frame would look out of place in a high-end
appliance commercial, the frame is wrong. The viewer should finish the scroll
understanding, without being told: *this company moves water through darkness and
delivers it perfectly into your home.*
